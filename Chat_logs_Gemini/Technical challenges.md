The Proposed Conceptual Mechanism: The "Fluidic Viscous Damper" (FVD)
The solution is a multi-layered structure situated between the Plasma Core and the Molten Iron Layer designed to transition the highly turbulent kinetic energy of the neutrons into a smooth, rotational force that drives the subsequent liquid metal layers (Molten Iron and Pb-Li).

1. The Shock Absorption Layer: The "Ceramic-Braid Mesh"
Structure: A thin, static shell made of a refractory ceramic composite (e.g., Silicon Carbide/Beryllium composite) permeated with a dense network of capillary channels.

Function: This layer is the first solid-ish barrier. The Directional Scattering Lattice (mentioned in the context) is conceptually embedded here. It acts as a directional sieve, causing the 14.1 MeV neutrons to scatter preferentially in the desired tangential rotation direction upon impact. The ceramic material also initially thermalizes (slows down) a significant portion of the neutron kinetic energy, converting it into heat, which is immediately radiated outward.

Turbulence Reduction: By forcing a directional scattering, the random shockwave force is reduced and replaced by a more uniform, though still rapid, directional impulse.

2. The Energy Channeling Layer: The "Liquid-Solid Transitional Zone"
Structure: A layer of the Molten Iron with a suspension of microscopic, spherical, highly conductive Ferrofluid micro-capsules.

Function: This zone acts as a fluidic clutch.

The directional neutron impulses from Layer 1 hit the micro-capsules, causing them to spin rapidly and uniformly.

Due to the high viscosity and magnetic nature of the surrounding molten iron, the spinning micro-capsules smoothly transfer their directional momentum to the bulk flow of the Molten Iron Layer through viscous shear and localized magnetic drag, dampening high-frequency turbulence while maintaining the overall desired rotational force.

Result: The kinetic energy has now been transformed into a smooth, low-turbulence, sustained rotational momentum in the Molten Iron Layer—the inner dynamo that generates the stable "muscle field." This rotational energy is then transferred to the Pb-Li Layer through standard fluid dynamics (viscous coupling).

Summary of FVD Action
The Fluidic Viscous Damper (FVD) breaks the problem into two stages:

Directional Scattering: Uses a ceramic mesh to convert random shock into a directional impulse.

Viscous Coupling: Uses a transitional liquid-solid zone to convert the rapid impulse into smooth rotational momentum.
